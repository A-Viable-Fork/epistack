# Where This Goes

This document states the destination, the first working step already built, and the phase that funding would complete. It is written in the same register as the rest of the submission: what is real now is marked as real, what is not yet built is marked as specified, and the future is described as concretely as the present, without a projection the work does not support.

## The destination

The destination is an ecosystem of markets for the components of knowledge work. A market of kernels to trust and fork. A market of surfaces to read and write through. A market of types, of connectors that bring source material in, of agent-producers that author through a checking gate. Each component is independently forkable, so no single market owns the others, and the ecosystem is designed to outgrow any single participant, including its first one.

This submission is the first market, the reference implementation, and possibly the first of many. The hedge is deliberate and it is the point: a commons built to be forkable cannot promise that its originator stays central, and a roadmap that promised otherwise would be describing a different, capturable thing. The destination is the ecosystem, whether or not any one participant remains at its center, and building it that way is what makes it worth building.

## The first step is already the artifact

The submission itself is the first working step toward that destination, not a description of it. It is a single surface a reader navigates the way an author navigates the commons: one canonical reading path, a fork to depth at every subject, the source of truth one click under every claim, and, at the end of the reading, the transition from reading to operating. Everything in it runs where the reader is, with no server and nothing installed, because the kernel, the contract, and the surfaces are all computed in place. An artifact that is at once a paper and a working system, forkable because it is a file, is the ecosystem's smallest complete instance.

What is built to that shape today: the reading and its grounding, the cases in depth, the generator that instantiates a kernel from a config, and the federation of four standalone kernels operated through a real contract. What is specified is the completion of that surface into a full on-ramp, the point where a newcomer builds their own kernel and federates it. The artifact is the destination's first instance at the maturity the status ledger records, no more and no less.

## The roadmap begins at a problem that exists now

The next phase does not begin with the commons in the abstract. It begins with an institution in acute, present, public pain from the exact problem this work addresses.

arXiv is being flooded with AI-generated submissions faster than its volunteer moderators can screen them. In October 2025 it restricted review and position papers in computer science, stating plainly that generative models had made such papers fast and easy to write and that the influx had become unmanageable for the moderators who are the preprint system's bedrock. By early 2026 it had introduced a one-year author ban for submissions carrying unchecked model output, after fabricated citations rose roughly twelvefold from 2023 to 2026. These are the actions of an institution everyone in this field respects, taken against a problem it has named openly, with tools that filter rather than ground because filtering is what it has.

The problem is precisely the one the kernel's core mechanism addresses. What distinguishes real work from confident generation is whether a claim stands when its author is subtracted, and that is the exact cut the kernel computes. A screen that asks whether a paper looks machine-written is a proxy; a structure that asks whether each claim is grounded in evidence a second party can recompute is the thing the proxy is standing in for. The flood is a trust problem, and the kernel is a trust-subtracting instrument, so the match is not opportunistic, it is the same problem stated from both ends.

So the funded phase runs in an order that solves a real pain first and builds the commons from the use it generates:

First, the ingestion edge aimed at that pain: a connector that pulls scholarly metadata (the arXiv and OpenAlex interfaces support this cleanly and legally, storing metadata and linking back for full text), and the grounding that lets a kernel hold a claim as grounded or not rather than as machine-written or not. The near-term deliverable is modest and real: a kernel that ingests a body of literature and distinguishes what is grounded from what is asserted, aimed at the moderation problem as it actually stands.

Second, the on-ramp completion: the manager and authoring surface that let a researcher build and populate a kernel without writing configuration by hand, so the first real users can act. The wedge is worthless without the tool that lets the people with the pain use it, so this follows the connector directly.

Third, the coordination fabric: the standing, gated-write, and challenge machinery a multi-author commons needs, built against the real use the first two stages generate rather than designed in the abstract. This is deliberately last, because coordination for users who do not yet exist is the performed-settling the whole submission argues against; the honest order is to earn the users, then build the governance their use reveals it needs.

Each stage produces something usable before the next is required, which is what makes the phase fundable rather than speculative: the money buys a working connector, then a working on-ramp, then a coordination layer shaped by real use, in that order, each de-risking the next.

## Why the commercial path aligns with the commons

The intended commercial path is stated plainly, because hiding it would be the less trustworthy choice, and because its shape is itself an argument for funding.

The way this work earns a living is by being the most skilled builder and toolmaker in a commons it does not own: authoring good kernels for organizations that need them, and maintaining the tools that make kernel-building easy. It is not a platform that charges for access, not a marketplace that takes a cut of others' work, not a hosting service that holds the commons on its own servers. Every one of those requires a center the architecture forbids, and every one of them is a position that forking destroys, since the tool that makes a commons forkable is the tool that makes any captured center abandonable.

That constraint is the alignment. The commercial path can only profit from a commons that thrives and stays open, because its income is the labor and the tools that a growing open commons demands, not the rent a closed one extracts. The incentive to keep the commons unowned and the incentive to earn from it are the same incentive, which is the opposite of the platform pattern where the two diverge the moment capture becomes possible. Funding this work funds the commons, because the work has no way to profit except by the commons succeeding on its own terms.

That alignment is the reason the hedge in the first section is honest rather than modest. A participant whose living depends on an open, forkable commons has no reason to promise it will stay central and every reason to build the thing to outlast its own centrality. The destination is the ecosystem, the first step is the artifact you are reading, the next phase begins where the pain is real, and the way it pays for itself is the same as the way it stays open.
