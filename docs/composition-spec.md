# The Composition Specification

Composition is how one kernel builds on another without fusing their schemas. A composite kernel cites claims from other kernels, domain stores or full kernels, across a thin protocol, and the protocol's whole content is a single shared type and the rules for crossing into and out of it. This document defines that type, the crossing, and the two operations by which a crossed claim earns local standing. It defines the mechanism; the argument for why federation is preferable to one schema lives in the judges document, and the three kernel types live in the kernel taxonomy, which references this document for how the crossing works.

## The untyped type

Every schema shares exactly one type, the untyped type. It holds two populations of claim, treated identically:

- Claims imported from another kernel whose local type does not translate into this kernel's schema.
- Claims a local author declined to type, for any reason.

These are the same situation from the protocol's side, because in both the protocol knows exactly one thing about the claim: that it is not grounded in the local type system. The protocol's stance toward both is therefore identical, and unifying them into one type is what keeps the protocol small, since it needs no theory of foreign type systems, only a place for claims it cannot vouch for locally.

The untyped type has three properties, and they are the whole of its behavior:

1. It is not a floor. Nothing grounds through it. A claim typed untyped has no standing on its own.
2. Inheritance is downward. A locally-typed claim that rests on an untyped claim inherits untyped status, because measured standing cannot be built on something the local system has not measured.
3. It carries origin as inert metadata. An imported claim records where it came from and how it grounded in its origin kernel's terms, and the grounding rule ignores this while a reader may use it.

Property one and two together are the laundering guard, and they hold by construction rather than by policy. Because the untyped type is not a floor and standing does not propagate up through it, a claim cannot import standing from a permissive kernel into a strict one; it arrives untyped and grounds nothing until it is forked and typed locally, which requires a named local author. So a cross-kernel launder is not blocked by a check, it is impossible to express, because the type that carries a foreign claim is the type that confers no standing.

Read against the definition in `docs/trust-and-view.md`, the untyped type is the one type that performs no attenuation. To type a claim is to subject it to that type's attenuation, driving its dependence on its author down toward a floor; the untyped type does none of this locally, which is exactly why it grounds nothing. It holds the claims the local system has not attenuated: a foreign claim whose attenuation happened under another kernel's rules, and a local claim its author declined to attenuate at all. That is the same fact the protocol already knows about both populations, said in the definition's terms.

## The crossing

A claim moving from kernel A into kernel B enters B as the untyped type. The crossing is lossy in one direction: it may lower or annotate standing, never raise it. A claim that grounded to a measurement floor in A does not arrive in B as a measurement in B's terms; it arrives as untyped, carrying the inert record that it was measured under A's rules. B may then treat that record as it chooses, as context, as weak support, or as nothing, but never as a floor of B's own.

The crossing preserves provenance and strips authority. What crosses is a record that something was grounded elsewhere, not a grant of grounding here. This is why the crossing is honest about disagreement rather than hiding it: a claim that has crossed three kernels carries three annotations and very little transferred standing, and that thinness is the true and legible cost of composing kernels that share no schema. Composition is the accumulation of such crossings, not the reconciliation of the kernels' internal grounding rules, which is what lets two kernels that genuinely disagree about what grounding means still compose.

In the definition's terms, the crossing strips the attenuation. A claim's standing in A is the work A's type did in driving out A's authors; that work does not transfer, because B's authors are other people and B did no attenuating. So the claim arrives with its local attenuation removed, carrying only the record that it was attenuated elsewhere, and the lossiness is not a defect to be minimized but the honest register of that fact: standing earned by attenuating one kernel's authors out is not standing over another kernel's, and a protocol that let it cross intact would be laundering unearned authority. The crossing is lossy because attenuation is local, and honoring the loss is what keeps the composite truthful about whose authors have actually been driven out of what.

Every crossing is recorded in the claim's history, so a crossed claim carries the chain of where it originated, which borders it crossed, and how its standing changed at each, and that history is bidirectional and traceable, which is what makes a composition auditable rather than a flattening.

## Earning standing: the two forks

A claim in the untyped type earns local standing only by a fork, and there are two, at two grains.

**Retail fork.** An author in the receiving kernel forks a single untyped claim into a local type, casting it into the receiving schema and signing the cast. The claim now grounds locally, because a named author asserted that the crossing is legitimate and staked their standing on it. The original untyped import remains, provenance intact, and the forked typed claim supersedes it locally. This is the common case, and it puts the cost of typing exactly where the judgment was made: no one had to agree that claims of this kind generally cross, one author decided this one does, and owns it. In the definition's terms, the fork is what re-subjects a crossed claim to attenuation: the crossing stripped A's, and typing the claim locally starts B's, driving the claim's dependence on the forking author down under B's own type, which is why the standing it earns is genuinely B's and not a transfer of A's.

**Wholesale fork.** When the same category of crossing is forked repeatedly, an author forks the kernel itself and extends its schema so that claims of that shape type natively from then on. The retail forks for that category stop being necessary. This is the heavier act, earned by repetition rather than declared in advance, and it is owned by whoever performs it.

Standards emerge from the relationship between the two. Retail forks are the leading indicator of where a schema wants to grow: a crossing that many authors keep forking by hand is a schema extension waiting to be written, and the fork history shows which. So a shared vocabulary forms bottom-up, as accumulated retail forks become a wholesale one, rather than being imposed by decree. The untyped type is therefore not only an escape hatch but the sensor that shows where a standard should form.

## The content-addressed crossing

The crossing above is lossy by default because it cannot generally tell whether A's type and B's type mean the same thing. When they demonstrably do, the loss is not necessary, and the mechanism that recognizes it is content addressing. A type is not a name but a bundle, the kind with its ceiling and rules, the floor with its rank, and the source class with its footings, and `kernel/schema/type-hash.mjs` hashes that bundle over its apparatus rather than its label (`hashTypeBundle`, over a canonicalized form so the hash is order-independent and deterministic). Two kernels that mean the same thing by a type therefore pin the same hash, and two that mean something different pin different hashes even if they spell the type the same; `build/check-type-hash.mjs` proves both the determinism and the meaning-sensitivity, and the built status is ledger entry [3.4].

The hash certifies which attenuation a claim underwent. The type bundle is the apparatus that performs a given attenuation, so its hash is the identity of that attenuation, and a claim carrying a pinned type carries a certificate of exactly which one drove out its authors. This is what makes native acceptance well-defined without trusting a name: the receiving kernel checks whether it pins the same hash, which is to say whether it performs the same attenuation, not whether the two kernels happen to use the same word, and can then accept the carried grade or re-ground the claim as its stance directs.

A crossing between two kernels that pin the same hash makes native acceptance of the carried grade available and well-defined. Because the target pins the very attenuation apparatus the claim already underwent, it can accept the grade without stripping it, and a composite in the target resting on an accepted claim grounds at the composed top. But pinning the same hash certifies the apparatus, not that the source ran it honestly, so acceptance is a permission the receiver may exercise or decline: it may instead re-ground the claim under its own execution, and what is guaranteed is only that standing is recomputable and that provenance transfers, never that the grade transfers automatically. A crossing into a kernel that pins no matching hash arrives untyped and grounds nothing, exactly as the default crossing does, until a fork re-subjects it to the target's own attenuation. `build/check-crossing.mjs` is the oracle for both behaviors, running four cases as independent kernels: a same-hash claim can be accepted native, its grade carried across (the accept-native stance, the current default), an unpinned claim arrives untyped, and a fork restores standing, so it is adoption of the hash, not the store boundary, that makes native acceptance possible. Whether a receiver accepts an incoming grade or re-grounds it under its own checks is its origin verification stance, a free parameter (`docs/parameters-register.md`), specified as a mechanism in the frontier subsection below.

This is where the degenerate lossless crossing named in the taxonomy below becomes concrete. The special case in which a crossing loses nothing is precisely the same-hash case: two floors coincide exactly when the two type bundles hash the same, so the lossless internal crossing of a top-down kernel and the available native acceptance of a same-hash crossing between two independent kernels are one mechanism, not two (native acceptance being lossless where the top-down internal crossing is unconditional, because inside one schema there is no separate source to distrust). The hash is what places every crossing on the one axis, from the identity crossing inside a single schema, through same-hash native crossings between kernels that have converged on a type, out to the fully lossy untyped bridge between kernels that share nothing.

## The origin verification stance, specified

The same-hash crossing frees a parameter it always implied but never named: what a receiver does with a grade that crosses in native-eligible. Pinning the same hash makes native acceptance well-defined, but it certifies the attenuation apparatus, not that the source ran it honestly, so a receiver that shares a type with a claim mill is not thereby obligated to absorb the mill's grade. The mechanism that expresses this is a per-origin stance applied to the `carried_grade` at the `citeDomainClaim` boundary (`kernel/composition/transfer.mjs`), transforming the carried grade before it enters the fold, keyed on `source_store` and leaving `compositeGrade`'s min-and-ceiling math untouched. Four stances: `accept-native` passes the carried grade through; `re-verify` replaces it with a local recomputation under the receiver's own checks; `discount` lowers it a band pending local confirmation; `reject` refuses the citation so the claim arrives untyped and grounds nothing.

This is admission policy at community granularity, the source-keyed sibling of the reader's inbound gate, and it is the parameters register's origin-verification-stance parameter (`docs/parameters-register.md`, `PR-origin-stance`). It is specified, not built: the grounding-layer crossing today copies the carried grade unconditionally, which is `accept-native` with no other stance selectable, so the current default is `accept-native` by implementation rather than by decision. The intended demonstration once built is the four-stance crossing demo, a mill community grounding a claim to checked under a shared type and four receivers admitting it four ways, accept-native, re-verify, discount, and reject, each recomputable. Whether `accept-native` should remain the default or the safe default is `re-verify` is an open design question this spec flags and does not settle.

## Forking a type and contesting a type

The two forks above cast a claim into a local type. Two tools bring the same motion down to the type itself: forking a type derives a new type from a parent, and contesting a type records a disagreement about one. They are additive over the primitives already defined, the type bundle and its hash, and neither modifies a crossing rule or a gate rule. Their reference implementation is `api/fork.js` and `api/contest.js`, and their oracle is `build/check-fork-contest.mjs`.

**The type fork.** A type fork derives a new type from a parent's bundle by a named departure and hashes the result (`forkType`). It is components-and-forking's one rule, a fork is a diff against a parent with its departures as coordinates, applied to the type bundle itself, and it is the crossing's fork at the granularity the crossing actually runs on. The receipt names the parent hash, the child hash, and the departure, exactly the fields the overrides changed with their old and new values, so a fork with no departure is the parent and is rejected as an error rather than returned. The crossing already governs the consequences: a claim typed under the child hash composes same-hash with a kernel that pins it, and arrives untyped everywhere else, re-earning by a local fork. Nothing is invented here, this is the same content-addressed crossing keyed on a hash the fork produced.

A type fork is snapshot only. There is no live fork at the type level, because a type-hash that changed under its adopters would break shared-meaning-is-shared-hash: a kernel that pinned the child hash would find its pinned meaning silently altered, which is the one thing the hash exists to prevent. The child bundle is frozen at the fork, and a later change is another fork with its own hash. This is the snapshot flavor of components-and-forking, chosen deliberately at the type level where a live overlay would be unsound.

**The type contest.** A type is a community's semantic policy frozen into checkable structure. Contesting it is a semantic act, judging whether that policy is the right one, which is the community's function and not the kernel's. So a type contest is an ordinary claim whose subject is a type-hash (`contestType`): it enters through the same gate as any claim, carries its own standing, and is answerable the same way any claim is. The contest claim takes an existing kind, `forum`, whose ceiling is the open forum band, the honest ceiling for a judgment about policy, which a contest can earn by independent agreement and never exceed; no kind is added to the schema. The subject type-hash rides the record's extension area, hashed and read by no check, so the kernel records the contest and adjudicates nothing.

The load-bearing invariant is that a type contest changes no grade. Admitting a contest leaves every existing claim's earned grade and certificate byte-identical, because a contest references a type, it does not support or contaminate any claim typed under it. Only a community's typing act moves standing, a fork adopted or a pin changed, and it moves it through the crossing rules that already exist. A contest that lowered a grade would be a claim-level semantic judge smuggled in through a side door; this invariant is the structural-versus-semantic attenuation boundary made mechanical at one more site, and `build/check-fork-contest.mjs` proves it by admitting a contest against a type in use and recomputing every prior grade and certificate.

Contest is voice and fork is exit, and the kernel structuralizes both without judging either. A contest's resolution is a community act outside the kernel: amend nothing, or fork (the contest's stated departure becomes the fork's overrides, so a sustained contest converts to a fork mechanically), or re-pin. When a contest carries a departure the type fork would accept, its receipt marks it convertible, and feeding that departure to `forkType` produces the fork the resolution would adopt. Who may resolve a contest, on what timeline, and with what stake is coordination-layer, specified in `docs/coordination-layer-spec.md` and not built here; the tools are the built substrate, the resolution authority is the frontier.

## The kernel algebra

The crossing and the two forks are operations on claims. Lift the same discipline one level and the kernels themselves become the objects: a small algebra whose operations each take kernels and return a kernel, so they chain, and whose one law is the no-grade-motion invariant carried up from claims to operations. This is the protocol layer of graph-as-object manipulation, the thing an interaction surface or a research console consumes; the built reference is `kernel/composition/algebra.mjs` and its oracle is `build/check-algebra.mjs`.

A kernel here is `{ store_id, state, tables, native, grades }`: a v3 store state and its reference tables, plus the set `native` of locally-typed claim identities and the map `grades` of each claim's grade. The `native` set is the crossing's untyped type made structural at the algebra level: a native claim is one this kernel typed and grounds; a non-native claim is a crossed, untyped claim that grounds nothing, so it recomputes to the bottom regardless of what it carried elsewhere.

**The governing law, stated first because it is the reason to trust the rest.** Every operation returns a kernel, and no operation assigns any claim a grade above what recomputation in the result kernel confirms. This is the gate's no-grade-motion invariant lifted from a claim to an operation. It holds by construction rather than by policy: every built operation fills its `grades` map by recomputing each claim's grade over the result's own structure (`recomputeGrade`, which floors every non-native claim to the bottom and otherwise defers to the kernel's own derivation, `kernel/store/decay.mjs`). An operation therefore cannot manufacture standing, because the grade it assigns is definitionally the grade recomputation confirms. The oracle checks the law independently of the operations, through the exported predicate `governingLawHolds`, so a break in an operation is caught by name rather than hidden behind the operation's own assignment; the deliberate-break test makes `project` retain a grade its restricted subgraph no longer supports and confirms the law fails naming the operation and the claim.

Three operations are built.

- **compose(A, B)**, built. The crossing as the algebra's product. B's claims and links are added to A, but only A's claims stay native, so every claim crossed from B arrives untyped at the floor and grounds nothing until a fork types it locally. The floor value is the crossing's own: a crossed claim recomputes to `ungraded`, exactly the effective grade `build/check-crossing.mjs` gives an unpinned crossing. The laundering guard holds by construction, since a permissive B cannot lift standing into a strict A when its claims are untyped by the operation itself. Compose is unconditional here: it floors all of B, the conservative crossing, and the same-hash native refinement (a claim whose type bundle A already pins crossing lossless) lives at the grounding layer's content-addressed crossing above, not inside the product. The app's type filter and the contestable dashboard are its downstream instances.
- **project(K, predicate)**, built. K restricted to the subgraph whose claims satisfy the predicate (by kind, grade band, domain or store, provenance, type-hash). A link to a dropped claim becomes dangling and is dropped, exactly as the crossing handles a dangling citation, so a support removed by the restriction lowers the claim it fed. Grades recompute over the restriction and can only fall, never rise: project never lifts a restricted grade. The worked case is a claim that reaches corroborated through a support and falls to asserted when the support is projected away.
- **diff(A, B)**, built. Over the shared claim-identity skeleton, the structured difference: claims present in only one kernel; claims present in both whose recomputed grades differ, carrying both grades; and contradiction surfaces, a claim graded above the floor in one kernel while contradicted or undercut in the other. It returns a diff-kernel whose claims are the difference records, itself readable and composable, and it asserts nothing it did not read: every difference record is a claim of a kind no kernel pins, native to nothing, grounding at the bottom. Diff generalizes the within-kernel profiling of `kernel/composition/profiles.mjs` from one store to the difference of two.

Three more are specified, not built, each with the grade behavior it must hold and the open question it carries.

- **overlay(A, B)**, specified. Diff's render: the same structured difference presented as one annotated kernel rather than a record list, for a reader to walk. Its grade behavior is diff's exactly, since it computes no new standing; it is app-layer presentation over the built diff, and the open question is entirely one of surface, not of grounding.
- **merge(A, B)**, specified. A union that, unlike compose, intends both kernels as peers rather than host and guest. It must floor the incoming permissive claims exactly as compose floors B's, or it launders standing; that much is fixed. What is unsettled, and why it is not merely compose, is the symmetric case where both kernels pin the same type-hash for a claim: whether a genuine peer-merge should let such a claim stay native from both sides, and how to resolve a same-identity claim the two kernels grade differently, is merge's own pass and is not yet drawn.
- **subtract(A, B)**, specified. A with B's contribution removed, the inverse of compose over the part of A that rests on B. Its grade behavior on a claim in A whose support was partly but not wholly in B, a partial-support removal, is unsettled: whether the claim falls to what its remaining A-local support earns (the project-style recomputation) or is suspended pending re-grounding is the open question, and it is named rather than guessed.

### The two structural laws

Two laws hold on the built operations, checked in the oracle.

- **Compose is associative up to hash** where the crossing is order-independent. The kernel's state hash is taken over the sorted claim and link identity sets and the sorted native set, so `compose(compose(A, B), C)` and `compose(A, compose(B, C))` hash identically: both carry the same claims and the same native set (A's, in both groupings), and the order of union does not enter the hash. Compose is not commutative, and the algebra does not claim it is: the host kernel keeps its native set, so `compose(A, B)` and `compose(B, A)` differ in exactly which claims stay typed, which is the crossing's asymmetry, not a defect.
- **Project distributes over compose** on an intrinsic predicate: `project(compose(A, B), p)` equals `compose(project(A, p), project(B, p))` up to hash, when `p` reads only a claim's own fields (kind, domain, provenance, type-hash). This is the characterized boundary the oracle draws precisely rather than omitting: distribution fails for a grade-band predicate, because compose floors B's crossed claims, so a claim that would satisfy a grade threshold inside B on its own does not satisfy it after crossing into the composed kernel. The failure is not a bug in project but a true consequence of the untyped floor, and the oracle reports it as a boundary, not a red.

### The grade-semantics frontier: kernel dialects, specified

The algebra operates over kernels whose grades share one ordinal lattice, the join-of-meets order with no arithmetic on the epistemic path (`kernel/schema/confidence.mjs`). That shared ordinal semantics is precisely what lets any device recompute a claim's standing identically without any two communities agreeing on numbers: the grade is a named position and its combination is a lattice operation, so recomputation is reproducible by construction, not by consensus on a scale.

A community that wants its grades themselves to be numeric, a Bayesian gate whose ceilings and recurrence are probabilities rather than ordinal positions, is therefore not asking for a core change and not asking for an algebra operation. It is asking for a **kernel dialect**: a kernel type with a numeric ceiling and its own gate, adopted by the communities that pin it and refused by those that do not. A dialect federates with ordinal kernels only through the crossing, and only as the crossing already handles a foreign type: a numeric-graded claim arrives untyped at an ordinal kernel's floor, and an ordinal claim arrives untyped at a numeric kernel's floor, because the two share no grade semantics to transfer. What stays true across the dialect boundary is everything the crossing already preserves without a shared grade lattice, claim identity, provenance, the crossing itself, and the untyped type. What does not cross is the one thing the dialect changed, the grade lattice, which is exactly why the two only meet at the untyped floor. This is specified, coordination-layer, and unbuilt; its home for who may pin a dialect and how it publishes is `docs/coordination-layer-spec.md`.

The frontier is worth naming precisely so it is not mistaken for the only way to hold quantitative epistemics, because two nearer homes already exist and cover most of what a reader means by wanting numbers. First, probabilistic content already lives in claims today: a posterior with its model and its data is a measurement-kind claim the gate grounds by its derivation, not by refereeing the number, so a Bayesian result is ordinary grounded content, not a dialect. Second, probabilistic analysis over the graph belongs at the algebra and console layer as views and drafts, a computed reading laid over the kernel, never a grade mutation. The dialect is only for the narrow case of a community that wants the grades themselves numeric, and even then it changes only its own kernels and meets everyone else at the same untyped floor every foreign type meets.

## The composability invariants

For a kernel to be composable, it must hold the invariants the crossing depends on, which are the required tier of the parameters register: claims are typed, including as untyped; grounding is monotone in the contamination sense; the untyped type grounds nothing; claims carry their history; and standing is forkable and revocable. A kernel that violates any of these cannot be safely composed with, because the guarantee the crossing relies on no longer holds. The register is the canonical statement of that tier; this document is why each invariant is required.

These required-tier invariants are enumerated as checkable entries in the contract register (`docs/contract-register.md`), the fixed-side complement of the parameters register, one entry per invariant here:

- `CR-typed` claims are typed, including as untyped
- `CR-monotone` grounding is monotone in the contamination sense
- `CR-untyped-floor` the untyped type grounds nothing
- `CR-history` claims carry their history
- `CR-forkable` standing is forkable and revocable

The boundary linter (`build/check-boundary.mjs`) reads this list and the ecosystem guide's invariants as the enumerable sources of the contract, so every item here must resolve to a contract-register entry or the totality check fails naming the orphan.

## Composition and the taxonomy

The untyped type is the coordinate that makes the kernel taxonomy a continuous axis rather than three discrete options. A top-down meta kernel is the point on the axis where the domains are regions of one schema, so every internal crossing is an identity, a trivial crossing whose losslessness is the special case where two floors coincide. A bottom-up meta kernel is the point where the domains are independent kernels and every crossing is a lossy untyped bridge. Each converges toward the other as schemas merge or relax, and a top-down kernel's native composition is exactly the degenerate case of this mechanism in which no crossing loses anything. The taxonomy names the three types; this mechanism is what places them on one axis.
